In this problem, you will build a decoding model that predicts the orientation of a visual stimulus based on neural activity.

The data come from experiments in which monkeys viewed static gratings of different orientations while the lab recorded activity of multiple single neurons in primary visual cortex (V1) using chronically implanted tetrode arrays.

The gratings were presented at 7 different orientations and two different contrasts (10% and 100%). Each grating was presented for 500 ms. This figure gives a sense of what a grating looks like and how orientation can vary (top row) and how contrast can vary (bottom row). Bethge et al. published a classic paper investigating the population code of these visual neurons using decoding analyses. We will implement similar analyses to what they did in this article: https://www.jneurosci.org/content/32/31/10618.short. Data publicly available from the Bethge lab here.

Problem 2a: comprehension check

Why does the spikes_test array have 119 columns?

There are 85 trials per orientation, and you hold out 20% for testing, so thats (0.2 \times 85 = 17) test trials per orientation. With 7 orientations, total test trials are (7 \times 17 = 119), which is why spikes_test ends up with 119 columns.

Answer

The coefficient matrix gives a compact view of how the decoder weights each neuron for each orientation. Positive coefficients mean higher activity of that neuron increases the models confidence in that orientation, negative coefficients mean higher activity decreases it

The magnitude absolute value of each coefficient shows how much that neurons activity influences the prediction for each orientation. Large positive or large negative weights mean the neuron is important for that orientation, values near zero mean it contributes little. A neuron with a strong positive weight for one orientation and weak or negative weights for others acts like a selective detector for that orientation. The pattern across the grid basically summarizes how the decoder uses the population to discriminate orientations.

Interpretation:

An immediate return to chance would reveal that V1 represents only currently present visual information without any persistence.

Research Question: To what extent is the V1 population code for orientation contrast-invariant? In other words, does the same readout, the same weighting of neurons, suffice to decode orientation at both low 10% and high 100% contrast, or does the population represent orientation differently at different contrasts so that a decoder must adapt?

Decoding Approach: I would use logistic regression to decode orientation from population spike counts, in a single time bin such as 195 ms, or averaged over the transient window, matching the approach already used in this assignment. I would load both 10% and 100% contrast data, same neurons, same orientations, same trial structure. I would then compare three conditions: 1 Same-contrast decoder: train on 100% contrast, test on held-out 100% contrast trials as baseline. 2 Cross-contrast decoder: train on 100% contrast and test on 10% contrast trials and vice versa. 3 Optionally, a contrast-independent decoder: train on a mix of 10% and 100% trials and test on each contrast separately. The key comparison is decoding accuracy of the cross-contrast decoder relative to the same-contrast decoder, like does the relative performance or ratio above chance.

Interpretation:

Outcome 1: Cross-contrast decoder performs nearly as well as the same-contrast decoder,more than 80% of same-contrast performance. This would imply that the V1 population represents orientation in a largely contrast-invariant way. The same linear combination of neural activities that works at high contrast also works at low contrast. Downstream circuits could therefore use a single, fixed set of synaptic weights to read out orientation across contrast levels, which would greatly simplify computation and supporting stable perception.

Outcome 2: Cross-contrast decoder performs much worse than the same-contrast decoder, near or only slightly above chance. This would imply that the population code for orientation changes with contrast, for example different neurons or different response profiles dominate at low vs high contrast due to differing semisaturation contrasts across neurons. In that case, an optimal readout would need to adapt its weights to contrast, or integrate additional signals that carry contrast information.
